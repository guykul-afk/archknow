# Accessibility Standard: Metric Handbook Accessibility

## Details from Metric Handbook, Page 61

4.4 Shared space 
The principles of shared space involve decluttering the streetscape 
by removing unnecessary obstacles and simplifying the area, all 
of which have benefits in terms of access and can add to inclu-
sive design. However, the removal of kerbs can be problematic, as 
kerbs provide many disabled people with the confidence that they 
are segregated from traffic. Kerbs can provide blind and partially 
sighted people with a ‘shore-line’ to follow, helping them navigate 
an area particularly in the absence of a clear and uncluttered build-
ing line to follow at the opposite edge of the pavement. Kerbs are 
also important, as they are required for taxi and bus ramps to be 
deployed safely, and for people stepping down from side access 
minibus transport. 
The Department for Transport’s Cycle Infrastructure Design 
LTN 1/20 replaces LTN 1/12 Shared Use Routes for Pedestrians 
and Cyclists. It identifies that a protected space, with appropriate 
physical demarcation, might need to be provided, so that those 
pedestrians who may be unable or unwilling to negotiate priority 
with vehicles can use the street safely and comfortably. Clearly 
defined safe crossing points should also be provided. 
There is a delicate balance between promoting active travel and 
creating schemes that protect pedestrians. LTN 1/20 indicates that 
shared use routes in streets with high pedestrian or cyclist flows 
should not be used. Instead, in these sorts of spaces distinct tracks 
for cyclists should be made, using sloping, pedestrian-friendly kerbs 
and/or different surfacing. Shared use routes away from streets may 
be appropriate in locations such as canal towpaths, paths through 
housing estates, parks and other green spaces, including in cities. 
Where cycle routes use such paths in built-up areas, they should be 
separated from pedestrians, perhaps at different levels or a kerb. 
Where shared space is proposed, early stakeholder engagement 
is a critical part of the design process. It should allow disabled 
and older people and representative organisations to be part of the 
design decision process. LTN 1/20 reaffirms that engaging with 
such groups is an important step towards a scheme meeting the 
local authority’s Public Sector Equality Duty. 
Careful consideration should also be given as to whether the 
area is suitable for such an approach, taking into account the vol-
ume of pedestrians compared to traffic flows, and types of traffic 
using the area such as bicycles, buses, HGVs and so forth. 
To support independence for more vulnerable pedestrians, con-
sideration should be given to the incorporation of a ‘safe zone’, to 
delineate optional routes specially for pedestrians only.
