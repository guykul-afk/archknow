# Accessibility Standard: Metric Handbook Accessibility

## Details from Metric Handbook, Page 54

1.3 Regulations and guidance 
In the United Kingdom, Building Regulations or Technical 
Standards form a starting point in determining minimal spatial cri-
teria for delivering accessible environments for non-dwellings. In 
England and Wales, these are set out in Approved Documents of 
the Building Regulations Part K and Part M, Technical Booklet 
R in Northern Ireland and the Building Standards Technical 
Handbook in Scotland. However, following building regulation 
provision does not necessarily deliver inclusive projects, as set out 
in the five principles mentioned previously. Here, the goal of cre-
ating aesthetic and functional environments that provide equity of 
experience for everyone requires a creative and inclusive design 
process that carries through the building design process and deliv-
ery into day-to-day use and management practice. 
A simple demonstration of this is as follows. The design of a 
new building incorporates steps that conform to the provisions of 
the building regulations. With the inclusion of steps, the design 
team add a ramp that also conforms to regulation, in that it pro-
vides step-free access. This ‘bolt-on’ solution is ‘accessible’ but 
not ‘inclusive’ as it separates those who can and those who cannot 
use the steps. An inclusive approach, for instance, might challenge 
if the stairs were necessary in the first place and if they could be 
eliminated entirely or ameliorated by changing the approach by 
gently grading the approaches. 
Good practice guidelines such as BS 8300 should be drawn 
upon in order to go beyond minimum requirements if an inclusive 
environment is to be achieved. 
For sport-specific approaches, see Sport England’s Design 
Guidance Note Accessible sport facilities with detailed commentary 
and spatial requirements. BS 8300 does not have detailed guidance 
for stadia. For development of stadia and similar venues, the Sports 
Grounds Safety Authority’s (SGSA) Guide to Safety at Sports 
Grounds (also known as the ‘Green Guide’) 6th edition should be 
used in conjunction with Accessible stadia and Accessible stadia 
supplementary guidance also published by the SGSA. 
Although withdrawn, the recommendations of Building 
Bulletin 102 Designing for disabled children and children with 
special educational needs are essential to incorporate early in the 
design process (it is worth noting that although the areas guidance 
is superseded by Building Bulletin 104, the general guidance is still 
relevant). 
Additionally, consideration must be given to local planning pol-
icy and any supplementary guidance or documents. For instance, 
the Greater London Authority set out a comprehensive approach 
to meeting their planning policy requirement for inclusive envi-
ronments with the publication of Accessible London: achieving an 
inclusive environment.
