# Accessibility Standard: Metric Handbook Accessibility

## Details from Metric Handbook, Page 55

• Sexual orientation. 
In taking an inclusive design approach, all protected char-
acteristics should be considered. Disability is the only protected 
characteristic that has specified duties relating to adjustments to the 
built environment and the need to remove physical barriers, as well 
as providing auxiliary aids and/or auxiliary services. 
The ‘reasonable adjustments’ duty within the Act imposes 
a duty on employers, service providers and public functions to 
make reasonable adjustments to any physical feature which may 
put a disabled person at a substantial disadvantage compared to 
non-disabled people. This is not a minimalist policy of simply 
ensuring that some access is available to disabled people. Instead, 
as far as is reasonably practicable, it approximates the access 
enjoyed by disabled people to that enjoyed by the rest of the public. 
The Equality Act is not a design standard, and does not impose 
design criteria. The Act does not have specific and defined provision 
for the built environment and therefore, physical features cannot 
be ‘compliant’, nor can any product be ‘compliant’. Although the 
Act does not cover physical features or auxiliary aids for protected 
characteristics other than disability, there is a general duty for all 
public bodies under the Public Sector Duty to have due regard to 
the need to advance equality of opportunity for people covered by 
protected characteristics. 
The design of built environment may create barriers to people 
with protected characterises other than disability, and therefore, 
further consideration should be given to delivering inclusion for 
everyone rather than ‘compliance’ with the more narrowly defined 
legal obligation. It is also important to consider the intersectionality 
of the protected characteristics set out in the Act, as a disabled per-
son might also be protected by one or more of the other protected 
characteristics as well. An example of this would be provision of 
accessible prayer facilities, or access to appropriate sanitary facili-
ties for people undergoing gender reassignment. 
The Equality Act does not apply to Northern Ireland, where the 
Disability Discrimination Act 1995 and the Special Educational 
Needs and Disability (NI) Order 2005 continue to give disabled 
people important rights not to be discriminated against.
