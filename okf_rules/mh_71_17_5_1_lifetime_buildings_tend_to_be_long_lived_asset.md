# Accessibility Standard: Metric Handbook Accessibility

## Details from Metric Handbook, Page 71

5.1 Lifetime 
Buildings tend to be long-lived assets, and often have considera-
ble operational costs, related to heating, ventilation and lighting, 
repair, maintenance and so on. Research undertaken by CeBe 
(Constructing Excellence in the Built Environment) identified that 
the cost of maintenance over a building’s lifetime can equate to 
many times the original construction cost. Furthermore, the costs 
of the operation, including the salaries of occupants, can equate to 
many times more than this. 
What these findings illustrate is that the lifetime costs of running 
and occupying a building will in almost all projects far outweigh 
the initial design and build costs. Therefore, if the design can be 
challenged to reduce operating costs or enhance staff productiv-
ity, the client may agree to invest additional capital costs to fund 
these improvements. More recently, this lifetime focus has been 
extended to consideration of sustainable and net zero carbon devel-
opments, both of which have operational cost implications and are 
forming a greater part of whole life assessments. Most notably, 
benefits to utilities usage are being considered in the lifetime cost/ 
performance calculations, as additional investment can provide 
financial benefit over the operational life, and inform payback and 
return on investment reviews. 
With the sustainability agenda very high up on many clients’ 
agendas, designers can be expected in the future to demonstrate a 
greater appreciation of long-term performance issues of their build-
ing designs. With the advent of the Government Soft Landings 
Building Information Modelling (BIM) strategy, the ongoing drive 
for sustainable development and energy efficiency, and the increas-
ing requirement for BIM enabled delivery, these operational issues 
and costs are becoming an integral part of the standard design 
assessment. 
It is important to clarify the distinction between life cycle costs 
and life cycle analysis (LCA). The former is concerned with cal-
culating the costs associated with the operation and occupation of 
an asset, and is the subject of this chapter. By contrast, LCA is 
concerned with the full range of environmental impacts of a build-
ing, covering embodied impacts, construction and operational 
effects, together with those associated with asset disposal. As well 
as greenhouse gases, the scope of LCA will include impacts on 
landfill, water, biodiversity and so on. Where these assessments 
can demonstrate financial implication/benefit, they can be included 
in the whole life cost assessment. 
The whole life cost agenda is potentially a very powerful tool 
for designers to create the case for investment in a design which 
promotes greater productivity, flexibility, durability or operational 
life. However, as with all forms of analysis, the whole life cost 
assessment needs to be prepared in a way which meets the client’s 
objectives, and which provides an accurate representation of future 
performance. The problems affecting the take-up of whole life cost 
analysis still include difficulties in obtaining unambiguous and cor-
roborated performance and durability data, and convincing clients 
of the long-term benefits to be gained from a more rigorous early 
assessment. The increasing desire, and in some cases requirement, 
for Building Research Establishment Environmental Assessment 
Method (BREEAM) and other sustainable assessment ratings is 
however driving an interest in whole life costing, as it forms a part 
of the available credits for these ratings. These trends, together 
with the increasing drive for sustainable, net zero carbon and circu-
lar economy solutions, are pushing lifetime financial evaluation to 
the forefront of many projects.
