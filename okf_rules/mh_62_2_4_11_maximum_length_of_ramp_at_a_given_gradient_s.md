# Accessibility Standard: Metric Handbook Accessibility

## Details from Metric Handbook, Page 62

4.11 Maximum length of ramp at a given gradient (Source: From Approved Document K) 
Legacy Development Corporation’s Inclusive Design Standards 
suggest a ramp no steeper than 1:15 should be used if a ramp is 
unavoidable. 
Slopes or ramps provide access for people who use wheels, such 
as wheelchair users or parents with pushchairs. However, some peo-
ple may find it easier to negotiate a flight of steps than a ramp and 
for them, the presence of handrails for support is essential. Stepped 
routes within the public realm can also offer shorter routes and help 
avoid people needing to travel extended distances that they may 
find challenging to walk along if only graded ones are provided. 
However, stairs are not to be dissected by ramps or graded routes 
as to produce feathered/tapered steps. Where a change in level is no 
greater than 300 mm, a ramp may be acceptable as the only means 
of access, avoiding the need for steps. Otherwise, steps would also 
be required. 
It should be noted that if the level change exceeds 2 m, then 
the ramps become very difficult to use for many people including 
wheelchair users. There should be an alternative means of step-free 
access such as a lift. Where lifts are provided, the quality of the 
journey should not feel secondary. Stairs can promote well-being 
and their use should be encouraged. However, lifts should not be 
hidden as a result. 
Lifts should have a minimum car size of 1100 mm wide by 1400 
mm in depth (classed in BS EN 81-70:2018 as a Type 2 lift). While 
complying with the minima set out in the building regulation, the 
use of this car size may preclude at least 5 to 10 per cent of wheel-
chair users and scooter users because of the length of the car. The 
maximum length of mobility scooters identified in BS 8300 is 1600 
mm. For this reason, it is recommended that a Type 5 lift 2000 
mm by 1400 mm (minimum) should be adopted – where space and 
budget will allow. 
The design of suitable stairs will be dependent on a variety of 
factors such as location (external or internal), building type, floor-
to-floor levels and other dimension constraints as well as whether 
they are fire escape stairs. 
Many people find long flights of stairs difficult to use; there-
fore, the maximum recommended number of steps should be 12 
in a flight and no more than 20 if a suitable going (300 mm or 
greater) is provided. If there are successive flights, it is important 
there are resting places, at least 1200 mm long (preferably 1800 
mm) across the full width of the stair. There should be an unob-
structed landing space at the top and bottom of a flight of stairs to 
avoid any collision. 
As a rule of thumb, a comfortable step will have a rise of between 
150 and 170 mm with a going between 300 and 425 mm deep. 
Stair risers should be closed, in part, because it removes the 
possibility of the front of a foot or a walking aide being caught 
underneath a tread during ascent, possible causing a fall, but also 
avoids the feeling of insecurity people get when looking through 
open risers on a stair. This therefore precludes the use of clear 
glazed risers. 
Steps without projecting nosings remove a potential trip hazard. 
If necessary, the projection of a step nosing over the tread should 
be a maximum of 25 mm. All nosings should be made apparent 
through visual contrast on both the riser and tread, which will assist 
people ascending and descending the stair.
