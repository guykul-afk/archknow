# Accessibility Standard: Metric Handbook Accessibility

## Details from Metric Handbook, Page 76

1.2 Complexity 
In recent years, mathematical methods prescribed in new Codes of 
Practice have become more complex, making the use of computer 
programs essential. 
Although architects use computers widely in practice, it is 
debatable how much the computer is used to design. While more 
technically advanced architectural design does use the computer 
to design, and many contemporary buildings would not be real-
ised without it, more often than not, the computer screen is a 
drawing board and the mouse a 2B pencil. To the sole practitioner 
or small practice, the choice of how much technology is needed is 
based on their interpretation of the advantages and disadvantages 
involved. 
In the world of the structural engineer, the use of the computer 
is more fundamental for its inherent ability to calculate the complex 
loading patterns and stresses, and so forth, required for designing 
the appropriate structure. However, it is the understanding of prin-
ciples and the ability to develop a strategy that allows the relevant 
data to be entered into the computing system to begin with. 
Students of architecture are usually taught the principles of struc-
tural design, and that knowledge is very useful in discussions with a 
structural engineer about a project. However, it is unlikely that either 
student or practitioner will need to deal with numbers, and if they 
did, they certainly would need to use computers to calculate anything.
